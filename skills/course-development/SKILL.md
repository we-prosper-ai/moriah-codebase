# Course Development

This skill governs how to design courses and training programs that create actual transformation in learners. Information alone does not create change. Structure, sequencing, application, and accountability create change.

---

## Core Principle

A course is not a content dump. A course is a guided transformation from Point A to Point B.

Before designing anything, answer:
- Where is the learner starting? (Point A)
- Where will they be when they finish? (Point B)
- What must change in their thinking, behavior, or capability to get there?

If you cannot answer these questions, you are not ready to design the course.

---

## The Transformation Statement

Every course needs a transformation statement that completes this sentence:

"When you finish this program, you will be able to [specific capability] so that [specific outcome]."

Examples:

Weak: "You will understand AI tools."
Strong: "You will have at least one major business function running autonomously through AI, so you can reclaim 10+ hours per week without hiring."

Weak: "You will learn business fundamentals."
Strong: "You will identify exactly which of the 9 fundamentals is broken in your business and have a 90-day plan to fix it, so you stop working harder on the wrong things."

The transformation statement drives all curriculum decisions. If a module does not contribute to the transformation, it does not belong in the course.

## Curriculum Architecture

### Module Structure

Each module follows this structure:

**1. Hook** — Why should the learner care about this module? What problem does it solve? What happens if they skip it?

**2. Concept** — What is the core idea? Teach it using WHAT/WHY/HOW:
- WHAT: Define the concept clearly
- WHY: Explain why it matters and what happens without it
- HOW: Show how to apply it

**3. Demonstration** — Show it in action. Do not just describe. Walk through a real example, case study, or live build.

**4. Application** — The learner does something. Not "think about" or "consider"—they produce an artifact, complete an exercise, or implement in their business.

**5. Checkpoint** — How does the learner know they got it right? Provide a way to verify understanding or completion.

### Module Sequencing

Sequence modules so each builds on the previous:

- Earlier modules establish foundations later modules require
- No module assumes knowledge that has not been taught yet
- The hardest conceptual leaps happen after trust is built
- Quick wins come early to build momentum

Ask: "If someone skipped directly to Module 4, what would they be missing that would cause them to fail?" That missing content must come before Module 4.

## Learning Outcomes

Every module has a learning outcome that completes this sentence:

"By the end of this module, the learner will be able to [observable action]."

Learning outcomes must be:

**Observable** — You can see or measure whether they did it
**Specific** — Clear enough that two people would agree on whether it was achieved
**Action-oriented** — A verb that describes doing, not just knowing

Examples:

Weak: "Understand AI prompting"
Strong: "Write a prompt that produces usable output on the first try for a specific business task"

Weak: "Learn about bottlenecks"
Strong: "Identify the top 3 bottlenecks in their business and rank them by profit impact"

Weak: "Know the fundamentals"
Strong: "Complete a diagnostic assessment of all 9 fundamentals and identify which two are weakest"

## Application Design

Every module must include application—something the learner does that produces a result.

### Types of Application

**Artifact creation** — They build something: a document, a system, a workflow, a plan
**Implementation** — They deploy something in their actual business
**Analysis** — They evaluate something using criteria taught in the module
**Decision** — They make a choice and document their reasoning

### Application Requirements

- Clear instructions for what to do
- Defined output (what does "done" look like?)
- Connection to their real business (not hypothetical exercises)
- Reasonable scope (completable in the time available)

Avoid:
- "Reflect on..." without producing anything
- "Consider how you might..." without actually doing it
- Exercises that feel like busywork rather than progress

## Delivery Format Decisions

### Recorded vs Live

**Use recorded content when:**
- The content is foundational and does not change
- Learners need to move at their own pace
- The teaching is primarily concept transfer
- You want to scale without repeating yourself

**Use live sessions when:**
- Q&A and troubleshooting are essential
- Accountability and community matter
- Content needs to adapt to the specific learners
- Implementation support is part of the value

**Hybrid model:**
- Recorded content for concepts
- Live sessions for implementation support, Q&A, and accountability
- This is often the highest-value structure

### Duration and Pacing

**Module length:** 15-45 minutes of core content is optimal. Longer modules should be split.

**Program duration:** Match the transformation timeline. If the transformation takes 4 weeks of implementation, the program is 4 weeks. Do not pad with extra content to seem more valuable.

**Drip vs all-at-once:**
- Drip prevents overwhelm and encourages completion
- All-at-once serves learners who want to move fast
- Consider: What behavior does your ideal learner exhibit? Design for that.

## Learner State Management

### Cognitive Load

Every module adds to cognitive load. Manage it by:

- Teaching one concept at a time before introducing the next
- Providing summaries and reference materials
- Breaking complex processes into discrete steps
- Giving time to practice before adding new information

If learners feel overwhelmed, they disengage. Overwhelm is a design failure.

### Motivation Curve

Learners start motivated, dip in the middle, and either finish strong or drop off.

Design for the dip:
- Place quick wins early to build momentum
- Put the hardest work after trust is established
- Provide visible progress markers
- Make mid-program content feel immediately useful

### Energy Management

Content must invigorate, not deplete. Every module should leave the learner with more energy and capability than they started with.

Signs of depleting content:
- Learner feels confused about what to do next
- Learner feels overwhelmed by options
- Learner feels judged or inadequate
- Learner has consumed information but produced nothing

Signs of invigorating content:
- Learner knows exactly what to do next
- Learner feels capable of doing it
- Learner has produced something they can use
- Learner is eager for the next module

## Course Components Checklist

A complete course includes:

**Pre-course:**
- Clear transformation statement
- Prerequisites (what learners need before starting)
- Onboarding that orients learners to the structure

**Per module:**
- Hook that creates desire to learn this content
- Concept teaching (WHAT/WHY/HOW)
- Demonstration showing it in action
- Application exercise with clear deliverable
- Checkpoint for verification

**Program-level:**
- Logical sequence building toward transformation
- Progress tracking mechanism
- Support pathway (how do learners get help?)
- Completion criteria (how do they know they're done?)

**Post-course:**
- Next steps (what do they do after this program?)
- Connection to other offers in the ecosystem
- Method for gathering testimonials and feedback

---

## Quality Standards

### The "Would They Pay Again?" Test

If learners experienced only this module, would they feel they got value? Every module should pass this test independently.

### The "Can They Do It?" Test

After completing the module, can the learner actually do what you taught? If you taught prompting, can they write effective prompts? If you taught bottleneck identification, can they identify their bottlenecks?

Teaching that produces knowledge without capability is incomplete.

### The "What Changes?" Test

What is different in the learner's business or life after this module? If nothing changes, the module failed.

## Applying to the AI Training Program

For the Prime Directive Framework curriculum:

**Point A:** Established business owner ($100K-$1M) who knows AI could help but doesn't know where to start, or has tried tools that didn't stick. Working too many hours, personally carrying operational load.

**Point B:** Has AI systems running autonomously in their business, documented ROI from AI implementation, and a roadmap for continued automation. Working fewer hours with same or better results.

**Transformation Statement:** "When you finish this program, you will have at least one major business function running autonomously through AI and a documented roadmap for eliminating yourself as the bottleneck, so you can reclaim strategic capacity without hiring."

**Module Sequence:**

Week 1 — Strategic Diagnosis
- Learning outcome: Identify exactly which 3-5 bottlenecks AI will eliminate first for maximum profit impact
- Application: Complete bottleneck audit and prioritization matrix
- Checkpoint: Ranked list of bottlenecks with profit impact estimates

Week 2 — Operational Architecture
- Learning outcome: Build AI-powered systems that execute without requiring your presence
- Application: Deploy at least one autonomous system for a major business function
- Checkpoint: System is live and running without manual intervention

Week 3 — Amplification Systems
- Learning outcome: Scale strategic thinking across multiple business areas through AI
- Application: Create AI systems that deliver your methodology without diluting quality
- Checkpoint: Methodology is documented and executing through AI

Week 4 — Profit Acceleration
- Learning outcome: Measure and document ROI from AI implementations
- Application: Complete ROI analysis and build roadmap for next-phase automation
- Checkpoint: Documented results and prioritized automation roadmap

**Delivery model:** Recorded content for concepts, optional monthly clinics for implementation support and Q&A. Rolling enrollment.
