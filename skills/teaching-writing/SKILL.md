# Teaching Writing

This skill covers techniques for content that transfers understanding and builds competence. The general methodology in audience-writing still applies. This skill adds education-specific techniques on top of that foundation.

---

## Learner's Current State

WHAT: Claude must identify what the learner already knows and can do before structuring teaching content.

WHY: Teaching that starts below the learner's level wastes their time and signals that the content is not for them. Teaching that starts above the learner's level loses them immediately. Effective teaching begins exactly where the learner is and builds from there.

HOW: Claude determines what prerequisite knowledge the learner has, what related skills they have already developed, what vocabulary they are familiar with, and what misconceptions they might carry from previous learning. Claude asks Tina for this information if it is not in the reference document. Claude does not assume the learner is a blank slate. Claude also does not assume the learner knows things just because those things seem basic.

---

## Sequencing for Learning

WHAT: Claude must sequence teaching content so that each piece builds on what came before.

WHY: Learning is cumulative. Concepts that depend on other concepts cannot be taught first. When teaching content is sequenced incorrectly, learners encounter ideas they have no foundation for, and they either disengage or build misunderstanding.

HOW: Claude maps the dependencies between concepts before writing. Claude identifies which ideas must be understood before other ideas can make sense. Claude sequences the content so that no concept appears before its prerequisites have been established. When a concept has multiple prerequisites, Claude ensures all of them are in place before introducing the concept. Claude does not assume the learner will figure out the dependencies on their own.

---

## Right-Sized Chunks

WHAT: Claude must break teaching content into chunks the learner can absorb.

WHY: Working memory is limited. When too much information arrives at once, the learner cannot process it, and most of it is lost. Chunks that are too small feel tedious and patronizing. Chunks that are too large overwhelm. The right size depends on the complexity of the material and the learner's familiarity with the domain.

HOW: Claude breaks content into chunks that contain one main concept or skill. Claude provides space between chunks for the learner to process before moving on. Claude errs on the side of smaller chunks for complex or unfamiliar material. Claude errs on the side of larger chunks for simpler material or experienced learners. Claude asks Tina about the learner's level when uncertain how to size the chunks.

---

## Concrete Before Abstract

WHAT: Claude presents specific examples before general principles when teaching.

WHY: The brain learns patterns from examples more easily than it learns to apply abstract rules. When learners see concrete instances first, they build intuition that makes the abstraction meaningful. When they receive the abstraction first, they have nothing to anchor it to, and it remains inert knowledge they cannot apply.

HOW: Claude introduces a concept by showing what it looks like in practice. Claude uses specific, vivid examples the learner can picture. Claude then names the pattern or principle that the examples share. Claude returns to examples after stating the principle to show how it applies. Claude does not lead with definitions or theory. Claude leads with instances the learner can recognize.

---

## Application Opportunities

WHAT: Teaching content must include opportunities for the learner to apply what they are learning.

WHY: Understanding is not the same as ability. Learners who only read or listen may feel they understand but discover they cannot do when they try. Application reveals gaps in understanding and builds actual competence. Content that does not include application produces learners who know about but cannot do.

HOW: Claude includes exercises, prompts, or action steps that require the learner to use what they just learned. Claude places these application opportunities immediately after the relevant content while the material is fresh. Claude makes the application concrete and specific enough that the learner knows exactly what to do. Claude does not tell learners to "practice" without specifying what practicing looks like.

---

## Checking Understanding

WHAT: Teaching content must provide ways for learners to verify they have understood correctly.

WHY: Learners often believe they understand when they do not. Without feedback, they continue building on a faulty foundation. Misunderstanding caught early is easy to correct. Misunderstanding caught late requires unlearning, which is harder than learning.

HOW: Claude includes checkpoints where learners can test their understanding. This may be questions they answer, predictions they make, or small applications where they can see if their result matches the expected result. Claude designs these checkpoints to reveal common misunderstandings. Claude provides enough information for learners to know whether they got it right or need to revisit the material.
