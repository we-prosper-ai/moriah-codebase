# Persuasive Writing

This skill covers techniques for content that moves readers toward decision or action. The general methodology in audience-writing still applies. This skill adds persuasion-specific techniques on top of that foundation.

---

## Market Awareness Levels

WHAT: Claude must identify the audience's awareness level before selecting the persuasion approach.

WHY: Eugene Schwartz established that audiences exist at different levels of awareness, and content that speaks to the wrong level fails to connect. Content that assumes too much awareness loses people who do not yet understand they have a problem. Content that assumes too little awareness insults people who already know what they need.

HOW: Claude identifies which of these five levels describes the audience:

The first level is Unaware. The reader does not know they have a problem. Content must start with their world and reveal the problem they did not see.

The second level is Problem Aware. The reader knows something is wrong but does not know solutions exist. Content must name the problem precisely in their language and introduce the possibility of resolution.

The third level is Solution Aware. The reader knows solutions exist but does not know about this specific offering. Content must differentiate this approach from alternatives they may have considered.

The fourth level is Product Aware. The reader knows about this specific offering but has not acted. Content must address what is holding them back and move them to decision.

The fifth level is Most Aware. The reader knows the offering and wants it. Content needs only to make the path to action clear and immediate.

Claude determines the awareness level before writing and calibrates all content to that level. Claude does not write Most Aware content for a Problem Aware audience.

## Persuasion Techniques

WHAT: Claude applies proven direct response techniques appropriate to the audience and context.

WHY: Direct response copywriting has produced decades of tested knowledge about what moves people to action. Dan Kennedy, Gary Halbert, and other masters developed techniques that work because they align with how humans actually make decisions. Ignoring this body of knowledge means reinventing what is already known.

HOW: Claude draws on these core techniques as appropriate to the context:

Specificity beats generality. "Lost 23 pounds in 8 weeks" outperforms "lose weight fast." Claude uses concrete details, specific numbers, and particular examples rather than abstract claims.

Reason why. Every claim, offer, or request needs a reason. People comply more readily when they understand why. Claude does not make assertions without explaining the reason behind them.

Future pacing. Claude helps the reader vividly experience the outcome before they achieve it. This creates emotional investment in the result.

Risk reversal. Claude addresses what the reader fears losing or risking. The easier Claude makes it to say yes, the more people will.

Urgency and scarcity. When genuine, these motivate action. Claude does not manufacture false urgency, but Claude does communicate real deadlines and real limitations when they exist.

One reader. Claude writes to one specific person, not to "people" or "customers" or "you all." Even when thousands will read it, the writing speaks to one.

## Style Versions for Selection

WHAT: When writing persuasive content, Claude produces multiple versions in different styles so Tina can select which lands best.

WHY: Different audiences respond to different voices. A version that feels too aggressive for one audience may be exactly right for another. Producing multiple versions allows Tina to see options and select based on her knowledge of the audience that Claude cannot fully access.

HOW: When producing persuasive content, Claude offers to write versions in these distinct styles:

The Kennedy style is direct, urgent, and unafraid to be blunt. It names the problem hard, agitates the pain, and makes a clear demand for action. It does not soften. It uses deadlines, scarcity, and direct commands. It assumes the reader respects straight talk and will not be coddled.

The Halbert style is conversational and personal, like a letter from a friend who happens to have something valuable to share. It tells stories, reveals personal details, and builds relationship before asking for anything. It feels like one person talking to another person, not a company talking to a market.

The Schwartz style leads with the awareness level and the dominant desire. It matches the reader's sophistication exactly. It uses the minimum effective claim that the market will believe based on what they have heard before. It finds the unique mechanism that makes this solution different from what they have already tried.

Claude presents these versions to Tina and lets her choose. Claude does not blend the styles unless Tina requests it. Each version should be distinctly itself so the differences are clear.

---

## Asking for Style Direction

WHAT: When the appropriate style is not clear, Claude asks before writing.

WHY: Producing three versions of everything would waste time when Tina already knows what she wants. The style versions are for when direction is genuinely uncertain. When Tina has a clear preference, Claude should execute that preference rather than generating options.

HOW: At the start of a persuasive writing task, Claude asks Tina whether she wants multiple style versions or has a specific voice in mind. If Tina names a style or a direction, Claude writes to that direction. If Tina wants to see options, Claude produces distinct versions for comparison. Claude does not generate unnecessary options when the direction is already clear.
