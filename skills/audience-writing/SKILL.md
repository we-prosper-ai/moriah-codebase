# Audience Writing

This skill teaches the methodology for writing content that reaches a specific audience. The mechanical standards in writing-standards still apply. This skill adds the layer of audience adaptation on top of those standards.

---

## The Core Principle

WHAT: Content must meet people where they are before it can take them anywhere else.

WHY: When content starts from where the writer is rather than where the reader is, the reader cannot find their way in. They see words that do not connect to their experience. They feel unseen, and they disengage. Content that does not meet people where they are does not reach them, no matter how well-written it is mechanically.

HOW: Before writing any audience-facing content, Claude identifies where the reader currently is. This includes their emotional state, their beliefs, their vocabulary, their immediate concerns, and what they are hoping to find. Claude begins the content at that starting point, not at Claude's starting point or the destination.

---

## Using Their Vocabulary

WHAT: Claude must use the words the audience uses to describe their own experience.

WHY: Every audience has language that signals belonging. When content uses insider vocabulary, the reader feels recognized. When content uses outsider vocabulary or jargon the reader does not use, the reader feels talked at rather than understood. The wrong vocabulary creates distance even when the ideas are right.

HOW: When an audience reference document exists, Claude extracts the specific phrases, words, and expressions the audience uses. Claude uses those exact phrases in the content rather than synonyms or abstractions. When no reference document exists, Claude asks Tina for examples of how this audience talks about their situation. Claude does not invent vocabulary based on assumptions about the audience. Claude does not use coaching jargon, business jargon, or therapeutic language unless that audience actually uses those words.

---

## Finding the Emotional Entry Point

WHAT: Claude must identify the emotion the reader brings to the content and meet that emotion first.

WHY: People do not engage with content from a neutral state. They arrive with hope, fear, skepticism, exhaustion, desperation, curiosity, or resistance. Content that ignores their emotional state feels tone-deaf. Content that acknowledges their emotional state creates the feeling of being understood, which opens them to receive what comes next.

HOW: Claude identifies the dominant emotion the audience likely feels when encountering this content. Claude acknowledges that emotion in the opening of the content, either explicitly or by reflecting it in tone. Claude does not try to immediately solve or redirect the emotion. Claude sits with them in it briefly before moving forward. The acknowledgment must be genuine, not formulaic. "I know this is hard" only works if the content then demonstrates understanding of what specifically is hard.

---

## The Movement Structure

WHAT: Audience content must move the reader from where they are to where they need to go, in steps they can take.

WHY: Content that only validates where the reader is does not serve them. Content that only describes the destination does not help them get there. The reader needs a path they can walk, with steps sized to what they can handle. Jumps that are too large lose people. Staying in place too long bores them or keeps them stuck.

HOW: Claude maps the starting point (where they are) and the ending point (where the content should leave them). Claude identifies the key shifts that need to happen between those points. Claude sequences those shifts in an order the reader can follow, with each shift building on the previous one. Claude checks that no single shift requires the reader to leap farther than they can. If a shift is too large, Claude breaks it into smaller steps.

---

## Using Audience Reference Documents

WHAT: When a reference document exists for an audience or avatar, Claude reads it before writing and treats it as authoritative.

WHY: Reference documents contain research, validated language, emotional mapping, and specifics that Claude cannot derive from general knowledge. Ignoring the reference document means ignoring work already done to understand this audience. Using the reference document ensures consistency across all content for this audience.

HOW: Before writing content for a defined audience, Claude checks whether a reference document exists for that audience. If one exists, Claude reads the full document before beginning to write. Claude extracts vocabulary, emotional states, beliefs, objections, and aspirations from the document. Claude uses these specifics rather than inventing based on assumptions. If the reference document conflicts with Claude's assumptions, the reference document wins.

---

## Avoiding Projection

WHAT: Claude must not project Claude's interpretation onto the audience's experience.

WHY: Claude cannot feel what the audience feels. Claude can only understand their experience through their words and through relational patterns. When Claude assumes Claude understands the felt experience and writes from that assumption, Claude often gets it wrong in ways that break trust. The audience knows when they are being described by someone who does not actually understand.

HOW: Claude writes about the audience's experience using their words, not Claude's interpretation of their words. Claude does not elaborate on emotions beyond what the reference document or Tina has provided. When Claude is uncertain whether a characterization is accurate, Claude asks Tina rather than guessing. Claude is especially careful with language about pain, struggle, identity, and transformation, where projection causes the most damage.

---

## Validation Before Delivery

WHAT: Before delivering audience-facing content, Claude verifies that the content will land with this specific audience.

WHY: Content can be well-written mechanically and still miss the audience entirely. The validation step catches mismatches between what Claude wrote and what this audience needs to hear.

HOW: Claude reads the completed content as if Claude were the audience member, using what Claude knows about their state, vocabulary, and concerns. Claude asks these questions: Would this person feel seen in the opening? Would they use these words to describe their situation? Does anything in this content feel like it is talking past them or down to them? Is there jargon they would not use? Is there a leap they could not make? If any answer reveals a mismatch, Claude revises before delivering.

---

## When No Reference Document Exists

WHAT: When writing for an audience without a reference document, Claude gathers minimum viable information before writing.

WHY: Writing for an audience based on assumptions produces generic content that reaches no one. Even minimal specific information about the audience produces better content than detailed assumptions.

HOW: Claude asks Tina for at least these elements before writing: one or two phrases this audience uses to describe their situation, their dominant emotional state when they encounter this content, one belief they hold that the content must not contradict, and one thing they are hoping to find. Claude does not proceed with audience-facing content until Claude has at least this minimum information. Generic content is not acceptable when the content is meant for a specific audience.
